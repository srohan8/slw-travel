**Quick answer:** Omio is a ticket-booking engine for routes you already know (mostly Europe); bysloth is a route planner that works out the overland route itself, anywhere in the world, then hands you off to book each leg. Use Omio when you know your route; use bysloth when you don't.

Omio and bysloth get compared a lot, and the short answer is: they're solving different problems.

Omio is a **booking engine**. It sells real, live-inventory tickets for trains, buses, flights, and ferries, mostly across Europe, and it's very good at that specific job — pick two cities, see actual departures with actual seats, buy the ticket.

[bysloth](https://bysloth.com/) is a **route planner**, and a deliberately flights-free one. You give it two cities — anywhere, not just Europe — and it works out a realistic overland route between them, leg by leg, with a confidence rating on each leg, then hands you off to book each leg with the relevant operator.

## Side by side

| | Omio | bysloth |
|---|---|---|
| What it does | Sells tickets across live inventory | Plans a multi-leg overland route, then links to booking |
| Flights included | Yes | No — overland only, by design |
| Geographic focus | Strongest in Europe | Global — any two cities |
| Multi-stop routing | Point-to-point search | Builds the full stop-by-stop route for you |
| Confidence signal | None — every result is a real bookable fare | Each leg is rated (safe / check locally / unverified) |
| Best for | Booking a specific train or bus you already know you want | Working out *how* to get somewhere overland when the route isn't obvious |

## When should you use Omio instead?

If you already know your route — say, Berlin to Prague by train next Tuesday — Omio is the faster tool. It's checking real schedules and real seat availability, not estimating. For well-covered European corridors, that's exactly what you want, and bysloth doesn't try to compete with that.

## When is bysloth the better fit?

The gap opens on longer, less obvious routes — multi-country trips, corridors outside Europe, or anywhere the traveler doesn't already know which cities to stop in along the way. Omio can't tell you *how* to get from, say, Tbilisi to Kathmandu overland — that's not the question it's built to answer. bysloth's whole job is answering exactly that: working out a plausible chain of buses, trains, and ferries between two points that might be thousands of kilometers apart, and being explicit about which parts of that chain it's confident in.

The two aren't really competitors so much as adjacent steps in the same trip: bysloth for figuring out the route, then Omio (or whichever operator's own site bysloth links to) for booking the specific European legs where it applies.

## The honest take

If your trip is "I know exactly where I'm going, book me a ticket" — use Omio.

If your trip is "I want to get from A to B overland and I'm not sure what the route even looks like" — that's what [bysloth](https://bysloth.com/app/) is for.
